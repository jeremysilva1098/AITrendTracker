Title: The 1xers Guide to LLM, ChatGPT and AI

Source: Pythonforengineers.com

Summary:

  • Large Language Models (LLMs) are computer programs that can understand, interpret, generate, and respond to human languages.
  • LLMs can be trained on terabytes of data and have billions of parameters, allowing them to generate intelligent responses.
  • ChatGPT is a popular commercial LLM, with ChatGPT 4 being the most advanced version available to paid users.
  • Open source LLMs, such as LLama 2 from Facebook, have gained popularity and offer free usage for certain limits.
  • Running LLMs locally on your machine can be done through tools like Ollama and Mozilla's LLamafile, which provide easy-to-use interfaces.
  • LLMs can also be used for image generation, but caution must be taken to avoid copyright infringement.
  • Following blogs like Ethan Mollick's and Simon Williamson's can help AI engineers stay up to date with advancements in the field.

Title: An open source DuckDB text to SQL LLM

Source: Motherduck.com

Summary:

  • MotherDuck has released an open source DuckDB-specific text-to-SQL LLM model called DuckDB-NSQL.
  • The model is trained on synthetic DuckDB SQL queries and general Text-2-SQL questions, making it capable of generating DuckDB snippets and SQL queries for analytical questions.
  • DuckDB-NSQL is aware of all documented features in DuckDB 0.9.2, including official extensions, and can generate the exact DuckDB SQL query you are looking for.
  • The model weights are fully released on Hugging Face and are also available in a quantized GGUF format for use with llama.cpp.
  • You can try out DuckDB-NSQL on the Hugging Face space and prompt the model with natural language instructions to get SQL snippets.

Title: RAG Using Unstructured Data and Role of Knowledge Graphs

Source: Kuzudb.com

Summary:

  • RAG systems can use unstructured data, such as text files or PDF documents, in addition to structured data.
  • Design decisions for RAG-U systems include what additional data to include (chunks of texts, full documents, or extracted triples) and how to store and fetch this data (vector indices or graph databases).
  • Knowledge graphs (KGs) can be used in RAG-U systems to link chunks of text through relationships in the KG, allowing for more relevant information retrieval.
  • The use of KGs in RAG-U systems raises the question of automatic knowledge graph construction, which is still an active research topic.
  • There are multiple possibilities for future work in RAG-U systems, including exploring the use of matrices instead of vectors, evaluating different graph-based heuristics for chunk extraction, and improving the extraction of high-quality triples from unstructured documents.
